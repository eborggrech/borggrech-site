I’m Edward Borg Grech, founder and CEO of [Bluefort](https://www.bluefort.eu/), based in Malta.

My career began in software development. Over time, I moved from writing code into the messier and more interesting work of building a company: delivering for customers, shaping products, hiring people, selling, making trade-offs and taking responsibility for outcomes.

## From software to company-building

I co-founded Bluefort and have spent years helping it evolve from a services-led software business into a company with its own enterprise products and recurring-revenue ambitions.

That journey has given me an unusually broad operating perspective. I have seen the same problem from different seats: as a developer trying to make the system work, as a delivery leader trying to make the customer successful, as a product builder deciding what *not* to build, and as a CEO thinking about markets, people, capital and long-term direction.

It has also made me sceptical of elegant theories that ignore implementation. Technology matters. But ownership, incentives, distribution, judgment and execution usually determine whether the technology creates lasting value.

## Why I’m interested in AI now

AI is becoming more than another productivity tool. Systems are moving from generating content towards planning, deciding and acting. That changes the organisational question.

The interesting problem is no longer simply, “Where can we automate?” It is also:

- Who remains accountable when a system acts?
- How do we verify outcomes without recreating all the work we automated?
- Which decisions should remain human, and why?
- How do organisations change when execution becomes abundant?
- What happens to agency, dignity and meaning when machines can do more of what previously made people economically valuable?

I am particularly interested in the idea of **accountable autonomy**: how we gain the benefits of increasingly capable systems without allowing responsibility to become vague or disappear altogether.

## Malta, Europe and the wider transition

I live and work in Malta. I see that as an advantage rather than a limitation.

Small countries can be unusually good places to test ideas: institutions are closer together, networks are denser and the distance between an idea and a decision can be shorter. At the same time, Malta sits inside the European Union and therefore inside one of the most consequential debates about how AI should be built, governed and deployed.

I’m interested in what Europe needs to *build*, not only what it chooses to regulate — and in how smaller countries can participate meaningfully in that future.

## Background

My formal background spans Business Information Systems, an MSc in Software Engineering and an MBA from Henley Business School.

The more useful education, however, has been building through real constraints: customers who need outcomes, products that have to scale, teams that need clarity, and decisions where there is rarely perfect information.

I write here to make my thinking more precise and to find people working seriously on similar questions.
