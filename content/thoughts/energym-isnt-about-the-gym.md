## Open with the real problem

The viral “Energym” video shows an imagined future where humans power AI systems through physical workouts.

It’s satire.

But it spreads because it feels emotionally plausible.

Not because we expect gym bikes to run datacenters.

Because we intuit something deeper:

If AI creates abundance, what do humans do with their energy?

And what powers the system that creates that abundance?

## Why this keeps happening

We talk about AI as if it only increases output.

But abundance changes scarcity.

When production becomes cheap, attention becomes scarce.

When answers are instant, meaning becomes harder to earn.

A world of abundant digital capability does not automatically create engaged humans.

It can create passive ones.

The Energym satire works because it compresses two fears into one image:

1) AI’s growing physical energy demands.
2) Humans needing structured engagement to remain meaningfully involved.

In *The Matrix*, humans become batteries.

In Energym, humans become participants in the system.

The question underneath both is the same:

If machines do the work, what keeps humans engaged in life?

## The tempting but wrong response

The first lazy reaction is dismissal.

“It’s just a meme.”

The second is dystopian panic.

“We’re heading toward exploitation.”

Both avoid the harder question:

How do we design a world of abundance that still requires human agency?

If AI reduces friction everywhere, humans don’t automatically flourish.

They drift.

Engagement doesn’t appear by accident.

It must be designed.

## A better way to think about it

Energym, intentionally or not, touches a profound idea:

In a world with abundant resources, humans still need a structured way to direct attention.

Attention is the new scarcity.

Energy powers machines.

Attention powers meaning.

If production becomes abundant, the challenge shifts from “How do we survive?” to “How do we remain engaged?”

That is not a technical problem. It’s a design problem.

Organizations face it already.

When AI increases productivity, the real risk isn’t unemployment alone.

It’s disengagement.

People need:

- clear ownership
- visible contribution
- feedback loops
- meaningful constraint

Remove all constraint and you don’t create freedom.

You create drift.

## What changes when you apply this

You stop designing AI systems only for efficiency.

You start designing them for dignity.

That means:

- humans remain accountable owners of outcomes
- AI drafts, humans decide
- outputs are reviewed, not blindly trusted
- roles evolve instead of evaporate

You treat attention as precious.

You protect it.

Because a disengaged human is not liberated.

They’re sidelined.

## Concrete example

Consider a company that automates 70% of its internal reporting with AI.

Option A:
Remove analysts. Let dashboards update automatically.

Productivity rises. Engagement drops. Insight declines because no one feels responsible.

Option B:
AI prepares drafts and highlights anomalies.
Analysts interpret, challenge, and contextualize.

Output speed increases.
Ownership remains human.
Attention remains active.

The difference isn’t technology.

It’s design.

## What I’ve learned the hard way

Efficiency without agency erodes morale quietly.

People don’t only need income.

They need involvement.

## A rule of thumb

In a world of abundance, design for engagement.

Because attention, not energy: is the ultimate fuel of human systems.
